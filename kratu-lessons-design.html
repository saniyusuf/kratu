Kratu · Alphabet Lessons · Design

Learning the letters, one small win at a time A gated, spoken curriculum that hears the learner and adapts — built for a Hausa-speaking classroom, running fully offline.

The alphabet is broken into small groups. Each group is taught, recited, and mixed up before the next unlocks — and it all happens by voice, with the tutor speaking Hausa and the learner answering with English letters.

01The board — the whole journey at a glance

Learners see the full alphabet as a board. Only the current group is open; finished groups turn gold. It makes progress visible and gives every completed group a small, satisfying win.

current group completed locked

26 letters · 7 groups of ~4 · progress saved on the device, so a learner can leave and come back.

02Inside one group — teach → recite → mix

Every group runs the same three-beat rhythm. Nothing advances until the learner has actually said the letters.

STEP 1

Teach

Each letter shown big, said twice the English way, framed in Hausa: “Wannan shi ne … A”.

→
STEP 2

Recite in order

Learner says each letter back, A→D. Right = kudos; wrong = try again; two misses = the tutor reminds them.

→
STEP 3

Mix it up

The same four, shuffled, to prove it isn't just memorised order.

On completion the group's cards flip to gold on the board, and the next group opens.

03The two tests

Main test

Recite A → Z

Once all groups are done, the learner says the whole alphabet in order — the proof they know every letter.

Final test

What comes next?

The board shows a run like O P Q R ? and asks the learner to say the letter that follows. This tests real recall of sequence, not recognition.

04A classroom you can feel — the colour & hand cues

The screen behaves like a patient teacher. The background and a raised hand signal, at a glance, whose turn it is to talk — vital for a learner who can't yet read the instructions.

05It speaks to each learner correctly

Hausa marks gender in address. The tutor uses the right forms based on the learner's gender chosen at the start — ka/ki, -nka/-nki — in both the spoken audio and the on-screen text, so it never sounds wrong.

06What every attempt records

The lesson quietly keeps score, so a facilitator can see who's struggling and with what. At the end there's a table: per-letter attempts, how many were right, and what the learner said when wrong.
